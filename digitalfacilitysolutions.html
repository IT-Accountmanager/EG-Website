DIGITAL FACILITY SOLUTIONS

        
2D layout has traditionally been the easiest method for factory design. But restricting
 oneself to 2D carries a lot of risks.
For example, collisions due to insufficient headroom are often only discovered
 during construction. The costs of modifying a design at this stage of a project are at their
 highest, and the associated delays affect the duration of the entire project.
 Misunderstandings can easily arise leading to costly design and construction errors.
Furthermore, when working in 2D, all views of a design have to be manually drafted, and
 then individually updated with each design change.
With a 3D approach, a single 3D design model is created, from which all the required
 drawings are automatically generated. This represents a considerable time saving over the
 life of a typical project, and also avoids the risk of introducing human error while manually
 drafting individual views



  
Digital Factory refers to the integration of digital technologies and
 data-driven systems into the manufacturing and production
 processes. It involves the use of advanced technologies such as
 Internet of Things (IoT), Artificial Intelligence (AI), robotics,
 simulation, and data analytics to optimize and improve various
 aspects of the factory operations. The goal of the digital factory is to
 enhance efficiency, productivity, flexibility, and quality while reducing
 costs and lead times.
  

    
Scanning for digitalization, in the context of the manufacturing
 industry, typically refers to the process of capturing and digitizing
 physical objects, components, or environments using 3D scanning
 technologies. These scanners use various methods such as laser,
 structured light, or photogrammetry to capture precise
 measurements and create digital representations of physical objects
 or spaces. The scanned data is then used for various purposes in the
 digitalization process, including design & engineering, reverse
 engineering, quality control & inspection, virtual simulation &
 analysis & digital twin creation.
    

  


Scanning for digitalization plays a crucial role in the overall digital transformation of manufacturing
 processes by capturing accurate physical data and converting it into digital formats.


  
This digital data can then be leveraged for advanced analysis, simulation, and optimization, leading to improved
 productivity, quality, and agility in the manufacturing industry.

 Digital facility solutions use data analytics techniques to analyse collected data and extract actionable insights from it.
 Machine learning models and advanced analytics algorithms can identify patterns, detect anomalies, and predict
 maintenance needs. Equipment uptime can be optimized, unplanned downtime can be reduced, and mechanical
 systems can be extended for a longer period of time with predictive maintenance

In order to reduce operating costs and improve energy efficiency, digital facility solutions concentrate on energy
management and optimization. Energy consumption patterns are monitored and analyzed, energy waste is identified, and
recommendations are provided for energy reduction. In order to minimize energy consumption while maintaining occupant
comfort, HVAC systems, lighting controls, and equipment scheduling can be optimized.

Digital facility solutions provide remote monitoring and control of mechanical systems within a facility. Facility managers
can access real-time data, monitor performance, and control equipment remotely through centralized dashboards or
mobile applications. By doing so, proactive decisions can be made, issues can be quickly resolved, and troubleshooting can
be done remotely. Asset and Inventory Management: Digital facility solutions help you manage assets and inventory. They
can track equipment usage, monitor maintenance schedules, and manage spare parts and inventory levels. As a result,
operations are streamlined, asset utilization is improved, and equipment failures are reduced.

Digital facility solutions include tools for optimizing facilities and managing spaces. Insights into space utilization, occupant
behaviour, and facility workflows can be gained from these tools. By using this information, facility managers can optimize
space allocation, boost workplace efficiency, and plan for future expansion. To provide a holistic view of facility operations,
digital facility solutions integrate with existing building management systems (BMS). HVAC, lighting, access control, and
security systems can all be controlled, monitored, and reported by this integration.
